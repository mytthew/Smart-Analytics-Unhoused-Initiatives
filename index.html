Overview

Laval & Roxanne
Liz Murray

Our analysis aims to provide insights into the trends of homelessness in the United States, and to offer recommendations for program administrators to better support individuals and families experiencing homelessness. We utilized data from the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) to analyze the Continuum of Care (CoC) program, which provides funding for homeless services. Our analysis focuses on the years 2007 to 2023, and we used machine learning to predict future trends in homelessness. We hope that our analysis will help program administrators make informed decisions to better support individuals and families experiencing homelessness.

Recommendations

Based on our analysis, we recommend that program administrators focus on the following:

Continue Supporting Homeless Initiatives
We're seeing a general increase in homelessness count from before COVID to years following, though local surveying suggests that there is still great improvement in the homelessness count in even more densely-populated cities such as New York, Philadelphia and Chicago.
Root-Cause Approach to Homelessness
While temporary solutions such as overnight sheltering and grants do offer some assistance, various evidence-based policies, coupled with insights from our model, demonstrate that root-cause approaches are highly effective in addressing homelessness.
Education & Public Awareness
By raising awareness about homelessness, we can address the misunderstandings and stereotypes associated with this issue. Increased awareness can garner greater support, encouraging more people to advocate for policies and programs that benefit homeless individuals and families. It's important to recognize that those experiencing homelessness are people with their own unique stories and challenges. Stereotypes often overlook the complexities of their situations, including barriers to employment and self-sufficiency, which contribute to their homelessness.

Data Limitations

Privacy
When working with individuals and sensitive data, privacy considerations are crucial to uphold. All data underwent anonymization to maintain privacy, thereby preventing the tracking of personal information and the analysis of individual cases over time.
Quality & Sampling Bias
The count of homelessness is conducted a single night in January (PIT or Point-in-Time Count) in emergency shelters, transitional housing, and Safe Havens, with direct contact made with each person surveyed. Although this surveying method enables greater accuracy and detail in data collection, it may be subject to bias and limitations in terms of the locations and time span it covers. This population is very mobile and those who were not settled the night of data collection may not have been counted in the PIT count.
Time
The availability of data during and after the COVID-19 pandemic is limited, thereby hindering our ability to accurately forecast future outcomes of the homelessness count. With more time and data, we could enhance our understanding of the evolving trends in homelessness and develop more accurate predictions for the future.
